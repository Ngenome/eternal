# Anthropic vs. the Pentagon: The "Supply Chain Risk" Designation
## A Landmark AI Policy and Constitutional Law Case (2026)

*Research completed: March 11, 2026*

---

## Executive Summary

The Trump administration's designation of Anthropic as a "supply chain risk to national security" is an unprecedented act in American history — the first time such a label, historically reserved for foreign adversaries, has been applied to a U.S. company. The action stems directly from Anthropic's refusal to allow its AI system, Claude, to be used for mass domestic surveillance and fully autonomous weapons. Anthropic has filed two simultaneous federal lawsuits alleging First Amendment retaliation, Fifth Amendment due process violations, and abuse of statutory authority. Legal experts rate Anthropic's case as strong. The situation has triggered a cascade of industry consequences: OpenAI rushed in to replace Anthropic, then faced internal resignations and had to amend its own agreement; xAI's Grok was fast-tracked into classified systems despite safety warnings; and the Pentagon has declared talks with Anthropic "over." A preliminary injunction hearing is scheduled for March 24, 2026.

## 2. What Is the "Supply Chain Risk" Designation?

### Statutory Basis: The Federal Acquisition Supply Chain Security Act (FASCSA) & 10 U.S.C. § 3252

The "supply chain risk" designation invoked against Anthropic draws on two legal instruments:

**1. Federal Acquisition Supply Chain Security Act of 2018 (FASCSA), Pub. L. 115-390**
- Enacted December 21, 2018, creating the Federal Acquisition Security Council (FASC)
- Established a **centralized, deliberative multi-agency process** for designating national security risks in the ICT supply chain
- Member agencies include: DHS/CISA, ODNI, DOJ/FBI, DOD/NSA, Commerce/NIST
- **Procedural requirements** the Act mandates before designation:
  - Conduct a formal risk assessment
  - Provide notice to the targeted company
  - Allow the company to respond
  - Make a written national-security determination
  - Notify Congress
- **Powers granted**: Exclude companies from bidding on certain contracts; require contractors to certify non-use of designated entities; facilitate removal of existing products
- **Judicial review**: Explicitly available, including D.C. Circuit review

**2. 10 U.S.C. § 3252 (DoD-specific procurement authority)**
- Grants the Secretary of Defense power to exclude entities from bidding on National Security Systems (NSS) contracts
- Applies specifically to systems used for "intelligence activities, cryptologic activities related to national security, command and control of military forces," and weapons systems
- **Explicitly excludes** "routine administrative and business applications (including payroll, finance, logistics, and personnel management applications)"
- Requires the Secretary to use the **"least restrictive means necessary"**
- Defines "supply chain risk" as situations where "an adversary may sabotage, maliciously introduce unwanted function, or otherwise subvert" systems

### What the Designation Actually Does (Narrow Reading)

Anthropic, Microsoft, Google, and Amazon all agree on a **narrow interpretation**:
- The designation applies only to Claude used "as a direct part of" Department of War (DoD) contracts
- It does **not** restrict defense contractors from using Anthropic for commercial purposes
- It does **not** prevent any non-DoW agency from using Anthropic products
- It does **not** force divestment or business disassociation
- Microsoft explicitly confirmed: "Anthropic products, including Claude, can remain available to our customers — other than the Department of War — through platforms such as M365, GitHub, and Microsoft's AI Foundry."

### What Hegseth Said It Does (Broad/Contested Reading)

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth publicly declared on X: **"Effective immediately, no contractor, supplier, or partner that does business with the United States military may conduct any commercial activity with Anthropic."** This far broader reading — effectively an embargo on all commercial activity — is what Anthropic's lawsuit challenges as an abuse of the statutory authority, which is procurement law, not sanctions law.

### Historical Precedent: First Use Against an American Company

Supply chain risk designations have historically been applied only to **foreign adversaries**. The first-ever public exclusion order under FASCSA (September 15, 2025) targeted **Acronis AG**, a Swiss company with Russian ties. Anthropic is the first American company to be publicly named. Legal experts note this is a fundamental departure from the statute's original purpose.

## 4. Why Negotiations Failed: The Two Red Lines

Anthropic maintained two absolute restrictions on Claude's use:

**Red Line 1: No mass domestic surveillance of Americans**
- Specifically concerned with "bulk acquired data" analysis — the exact phrase the Pentagon offered to accept but then asked Anthropic to delete
- Amodei said the deletion of this phrase showed the government intended to use AI for exactly what Anthropic feared

**Red Line 2: No fully autonomous weapons (lethal autonomy without human authorization)**
- Anthropic's position: "Today's frontier AI models are not reliable enough to be used in fully autonomous weapons"
- Pentagon's position: Existing laws and internal policies already prohibit autonomous weapons; AI contractors shouldn't insert themselves into the chain of command

### Pentagon's Final Offer (Rejected)
Emil Michael offered **written acknowledgments of existing federal laws** restricting surveillance and autonomous weapons, stating "at some level, you have to trust your military to do the right thing." Anthropic deemed this inadequate — the acknowledgments didn't add new restrictions, just cited current law, which the Pentagon could change.

### Amodei's Internal Memo
Amodei reportedly told Anthropic staff the OpenAI deal was "safety theater" and the messaging "straight up lies." He suggested the relationship soured because Anthropic hadn't engaged in "dictator-style praise" or major donations to the administration.

## 5. The Two Lawsuits

### Lawsuit 1: N.D. California (Primary Case)
- **Case**: *Anthropic PBC v. U.S. Department of War*, Case No. 3:26-cv-01996
- **Court**: U.S. District Court, Northern District of California (San Francisco)
- **Judge**: **Rita F. Lin**
- **Filed**: March 9, 2026
- **Defendants**: U.S. Department of War, DoD, Treasury, State, General Services Administration, and **over a dozen other federal agencies**

**Legal Claims:**
1. **First Amendment Retaliation** — The designation punishes Anthropic for its public speech on AI safety policy, including advocacy against mass surveillance and autonomous weapons. "The Constitution does not allow the government to wield its enormous power to punish a company for its protected speech."
2. **Fifth Amendment Due Process** — The designation was issued "without adequate notice and a meaningful hearing," imposing severe penalties without opportunity to challenge or present evidence
3. **Administrative Procedure Act (APA)** — The action is "arbitrary, capricious, and an abuse of discretion," "unsupported by the administrative record," and issued "not in accord with procedures required by law"
4. **Excess of Statutory Authority** — "No federal statute authorizes the actions taken here"; the FASCSA procedural requirements (risk assessment, notice, response opportunity, written determination, Congressional notification) were not followed

**Relief Sought:**
- Undo the supply chain risk designation
- Block its enforcement
- Require all federal agencies to withdraw directives to drop Anthropic

### Lawsuit 2: D.C. Circuit Court of Appeals
- **Court**: U.S. Court of Appeals for the D.C. Circuit
- **Filed**: March 9, 2026 (same day as N.D. Cal. case)
- **Purpose**: Filed because FASCSA provides that certain designations can **only be challenged** in the D.C. Circuit
- **Claims**: Substantially similar to N.D. Cal. case — First Amendment retaliation, Fifth Amendment due process, APA violations, excess of statutory authority
- **Key Argument**: "The Department of War's actions are, among other things, a pretextual form of retaliation in violation of the First and Fifth Amendments to the U.S. Constitution; arbitrary, capricious, and an abuse of discretion; unsupported by the administrative record; not in accord with procedures required by law; and in excess of statutory authority"

## 7. OpenAI's Original Deal and What Was Amended

### The Original Deal (February 27, 2026)
- OpenAI announced an agreement with the Pentagon to deploy ChatGPT in classified military environments **within hours** of Trump's ban on Anthropic
- The deal covered use of OpenAI's services in classified settings, positioned as a direct replacement for Anthropic
- **No specific safeguards** around mass surveillance or autonomous weapons were publicly announced in the original announcement
- The announcement was made without defined protective measures — Kalinowski's central criticism

### Public Backlash
- ChatGPT app saw **~300% increase in uninstall rates** in the day after the deal was announced
- Hundreds of OpenAI and Google employees had signed an open letter demanding limits on AI use for surveillance and autonomous weapons
- Claude surpassed ChatGPT as the top AI app in Apple's App Store in 20+ countries

### The Amended Agreement
Sam Altman acknowledged the company "shouldn't have rushed" and that the deal "looked opportunistic and sloppy." The amended agreement added:
- Language "consistent with applicable laws"
- Explicit declaration that **"AI system shall not be intentionally used for domestic surveillance of U.S. persons and nationals"**
- Language around autonomous weapons

Altman stated: The agreement "creates a workable path for responsible national security uses of AI while making clear our red lines: no domestic surveillance and no autonomous weapons."

### Caitlin Kalinowski's Resignation (March 7, 2026)
- **Most senior OpenAI executive** to publicly break with the company over the Pentagon deal
- Led OpenAI's hardware and robotics teams since November 2024
- Her statement: *"Surveillance of Americans without judicial oversight and lethal autonomy without human authorization are lines that deserved more deliberation than they got. This was about principle, not people."*
- Follow-up: *"The announcement was rushed without the guardrails defined. It's a governance concern first and foremost."*
- Also expressed respect for Altman personally but said process concerns were paramount

## 9. What Legal Experts Are Saying

### Overall Assessment: Anthropic Has a Strong Case

The legal consensus as of March 11, 2026 is that Anthropic has strong arguments across multiple legal theories:

**Michael Pastor (New York Law School, former NYC General Counsel):**
> "Anthropic makes several strong First Amendment and due process arguments in a case that has 'escalated beyond comprehension.' I've never seen a case like this."

**Joel Dodge (Vanderbilt University Law):**
> "A lot of things Hegseth has said and the Pentagon has done undermine their case and suggest there was personal animus and bad blood between the parties, and that the Pentagon had it out for Anthropic."
> — Note: Trump's own public posts calling Anthropic a "RADICAL LEFT WOKE COMPANY" are cited as direct evidence of viewpoint-based targeting.

**Alan Rozenshtein (University of Minnesota Law School):**
> "The government was simultaneously threatening to use the [Defense Production Act] to force Anthropic to sell its services, using its services in active military operations, and saying it's too dangerous to use them in government contracts."
> — The contradiction of continuing to use Claude in active Iran operations while claiming it's a supply chain risk is seen as a key vulnerability in the government's position.

**Eric Crusius (government contracts attorney):**
> The government is trying to impose the "death penalty" on Anthropic and will need to show "there was no alternative and that they meticulously considered other options prior to pulling the trigger."

**Carl Tobias (University of Richmond School of Law):**
> The Trump administration will take "a scorched earth" approach; the case will likely reach the **Supreme Court**.

### Core Legal Vulnerabilities in the Government's Position

1. **Misuse of procurement statute as sanctions tool**: Section 3252 is procurement authority, not sanctions authority. Hegseth's broad public statement prohibiting all commercial activity exceeds the statute's scope.

2. **Procedural failures**: FASCSA requires: risk assessment → notice → opportunity to respond → written national security determination → Congressional notification. None of these appear to have been followed.

3. **Definitional mismatch**: The statutory definition of "supply chain risk" requires evidence that "an adversary may sabotage, maliciously introduce unwanted function, or otherwise subvert" systems. Both parties acknowledged negotiations broke down over **terms of use**, not adversarial behavior. There's no allegation that Anthropic is a foreign adversary.

4. **First Amendment retaliation**: The government is punishing a company for its public advocacy positions. The Supreme Court has established robust protections against government retaliation for protected speech.

5. **Irony of concurrent use**: The U.S. military is actively using Claude in its Iran campaign (embedded in Palantir's Maven Smart System) while simultaneously designating it a national security risk.

6. **"Least restrictive means" requirement**: 10 U.S.C. § 3252 requires the Secretary to use the least restrictive means. A blanket commercial embargo is arguably not that.

## 11. Constitutional and Statutory Framework at Stake

### First Amendment (Free Speech / Retaliation)
The government cannot penalize companies for their public advocacy positions. Anthropic's "protected speech" includes:
- Public statements about the limitations of AI for autonomous weapons
- Advocacy for AI safety guardrails
- CEO's public positions on AI policy

The government's counter-argument is that this is about "operational control," not speech — the Pentagon should be able to deploy purchased technology without vendor interference.

### Fifth Amendment (Due Process)
Anthropic was designated without:
- Adequate notice
- Meaningful opportunity to respond
- A formal hearing
- A written determination explaining the basis

### Administrative Procedure Act (APA)
Courts can overturn agency actions that are:
- Arbitrary and capricious
- Not supported by substantial evidence
- Contrary to law
- Without observance of required procedures

### FASCSA Judicial Review Provision
The law explicitly provides for judicial review — and specifically, that certain orders may only be challenged in the D.C. Circuit, which is why Anthropic filed both cases simultaneously.

### Defense Production Act Threat
The administration reportedly discussed invoking the **Defense Production Act** to force Anthropic to provide services — a threat Amodei cited as evidence of bad faith.

## 15. Significance: Why This Case Matters

This case represents the intersection of several landmark questions:

1. **Can the government weaponize procurement authority to silence corporate speech?** The supply chain risk statute was designed for foreign adversaries. Its use here is a fundamental expansion.

2. **What are AI companies' rights when governments demand removal of safety guardrails?** This is the first case to test whether safety-motivated AI restrictions are protected speech.

3. **Who controls AI deployment decisions — developers or government customers?** The Pentagon's "any lawful purpose" demand vs. Anthropic's red lines frames a constitutional question about the limits of government procurement power.

4. **Will AI safety considerations be viable in a national security context?** If Anthropic loses, no AI company will be able to maintain safety restrictions on government customers. If it wins, a new precedent is set for AI ethics as protected advocacy.

5. **Innovation chilling effects**: The designation of an American company as a national security risk for disagreeing with its government customer could reshape how all technology companies approach government contracts and public safety advocacy.
